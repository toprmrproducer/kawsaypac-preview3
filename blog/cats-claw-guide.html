Cat's Claw (Una de Gato): The Complete Amazonian Guide

June 17, 2026 · 3 min read · By the Kawsaypac team

Walk any Amazonian forest trail with a local harvester and eventually they will stop at a woody vine climbing some giant tree, point out the curved thorns that give it its name, and tell you their family has been drinking its bark for as long as anyone remembers. Uncaria tomentosa, una de gato, cat's claw: one of the most storied plants in South American herbalism. This guide covers the botany, the tradition, and the two things Americans most often get wrong with it, preparation and sourcing.

What cat's claw is

Cat's claw is a climbing woody vine of the Amazon basin that can run thirty meters up into the canopy, anchoring itself with paired curved thorns that look exactly like their namesake. The herb of commerce is the inner bark, harvested in strips from mature vines, dried, and traditionally simmered into a rich red-brown decoction. Our pouches contain that bark whole, in visible curls and shavings, never powdered. It has a woody, earthy, gently astringent character, more forest floor than flower garden, and it is one of those plants whose taste tells you it means business.

The tradition behind it

Among Amazonian peoples, cat's claw bark has centuries of documented use, most consistently in the territory of joint comfort, resilience, and what modern language would call immune support. It is a maintenance plant in the traditional pattern: decocted in quantity, drunk in courses, part of the household repertoire for elders and hard-working bodies in particular. We repeat our standing line here with extra emphasis because this plant attracts extravagant internet claims: tradition is not clinical proof, we make no medical promises, and anyone managing a condition or taking immune-active or blood medications should talk to a clinician before adding cat's claw to anything. Respecting a plant includes respecting its seriousness.

How to prepare it, properly

Here is the mistake to avoid: steeping bark like tea. A five-minute infusion barely introduces itself to material this dense. Cat's claw wants a true decoction. Use roughly a tablespoon of bark per two cups of water, bring to a gentle simmer, lid mostly on, and hold it there for fifteen to twenty minutes, until the liquid runs deep reddish brown. Strain and drink warm, one to two cups a day in courses of a few weeks with rest periods between, the traditional cycling pattern. The same bark will stand a second, longer simmer. If decoction is new territory, our Herbal Preparation Book walks through it step by step; this plant alone justifies learning the method.

Who it is for

Cat's claw suits the person building a considered joint-and-resilience ritual, particularly athletes, lifters, tradespeople, and anyone whose body logs heavy mileage. It suits herbal practitioners who want serious raw material rather than capsule dust. And it suits the tradition-minded drinker who wants one legendary plant, unblended, prepared the old way. It is not a quick fix, not a painkiller, and its territory overlaps interestingly with chuchuhuasi, the other great Amazonian bark, which many of our customers run in alternating courses.

Why ours is different

Cat's claw's fame created a commodity market, and the commodity market did what it does: powders of uncertain species, dubious plant parts, bark from over-harvested wild stands stripped without care. Ours comes from Indigenous partner harvesters in the Ecuadorian Amazon who take bark selectively from mature vines, without killing them, on rotations that let stands recover, exactly the practice we described in wildcrafted versus commercial herbs. Whole bark, named origin, small imported batches, direct pay at the source, shipped fresh across America. For a plant this revered, we think that is the only version worth selling, and the only version worth your twenty minutes at the stove.

Common questions

Does the decoction taste terrible? No, just serious: woody, earthy, mildly tannic, closer to a forest broth than a floral tea. Most people acclimate within a few cups and come to like its gravity. A slice of ginger simmered alongside is the traditional softener and a good one.

Capsules would be easier. Why bark? Because with capsules you are trusting a powder you cannot inspect, from a species you cannot verify, at an age you cannot guess. Whole bark shows you everything, and the twenty-minute simmer is the price of that certainty. We think the trade is obvious, and we say so as people who could sell capsules tomorrow.
